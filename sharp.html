Candidate pathfinder mission

SHARP: a pathfinder for space-based black-hole imaging.

SHARP is a proposed mission concept for testing the space-VLBI architecture, timing, baseline reconstruction, data handling, correlation, and imaging capabilities needed for a future Space Array observatory.

Focused pathfinder

A concrete step toward space-based very-long-baseline interferometry.

SHARP can be framed as a pathfinder mission concept: focused enough to define measurable requirements, but ambitious enough to test the core architecture needed for a future Space Array observatory.

Its role is to connect black-hole imaging goals to spacecraft formation, timing, baseline reconstruction, data handling, correlation, and imaging requirements.

Mission concept parameters

Connecting black-hole science goals to system-level requirements.

SHARP can be described through a small set of mission parameters that link scientific ambition to architecture, operations, and technology demonstrations.

Science target

Compact black-hole systems

Focused high-resolution observations of event-horizon-scale structure, accretion flows, photon-ring signatures, and jet-launching regions.

Interferometric mode

Space very-long-baseline interferometry

Long baselines beyond Earth-scale limits, designed to extend angular resolution and uv coverage for compact targets.

Space segment

Focused spacecraft formation

A pathfinder-scale architecture that can test relative navigation, baseline reconstruction, pointing, and coordinated observations.

Critical demonstration

End-to-end phase preservation

Demonstrating that timing, receiver stability, orbit knowledge, data handling, correlation, and calibration can work as one system.

Data system

Transport, correlation, and imaging

Moving high-volume observations from spacecraft to processing systems, then producing calibrated interferometric measurements and images.

Path to Space Array

Architecture validation

SHARP would reduce risk for a larger Space Array by validating the core observing chain before scaling to an observatory-class system.
