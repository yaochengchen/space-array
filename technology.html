Technology roadmap

From enabling technologies to an operational array.

The Space Array initiative connects science requirements to the technologies needed for formation-flying interferometry in space.

System architecture

An observatory built from synchronized spacecraft, links, and computation.

Space Array depends on more than individual spacecraft. It requires a coordinated architecture in which formation flying, timing, receivers, optical links, correlation, and imaging operate as a single interferometric instrument.

The technology roadmap therefore connects physical spacecraft capabilities to the information flow needed for science: collecting signals, preserving phase, transporting data, reconstructing baselines, and producing images.

Technical challenges

Critical problems that define the path to a space array.

A future space-based interferometer must solve a coupled set of challenges across spacecraft engineering, timing, data systems, calibration, and astronomical imaging.

01

Baseline knowledge

The relative position of each spacecraft must be reconstructed with enough precision to support phase calibration and high-resolution interferometric imaging.

02

Phase coherence

Clock stability, time transfer, receiver phase stability, and calibration strategy must work together to preserve coherence over long baselines.

03

Data volume

Space VLBI observations can generate large data rates, requiring careful choices between onboard storage, compression, inter-satellite links, and downlink capacity.

04

Sparse uv coverage

A small number of spacecraft produces incomplete sampling of spatial frequencies, making orbit design, observing strategy, and image reconstruction tightly coupled.

05

Autonomous operations

Distributed spacecraft must coordinate observations, pointing, timing, data handling, and fault responses with limited real-time intervention from the ground.

06

Space qualification

Receivers, clocks, optical links, storage systems, and processing hardware must be adapted for the thermal, radiation, power, and reliability constraints of space.

Technology readiness

A staged path from concept studies to an operational observatory.

The Space Array technology programme can be framed as a sequence of increasingly integrated demonstrations, each reducing risk for a future space-based interferometric observatory.

Phase 01

Concept and simulation

Define science requirements, candidate orbits, observing bands, baseline needs, uv coverage, data rates, and imaging performance through end-to-end simulations.

  • Science-to-system requirements
  • Orbit and baseline studies
  • Synthetic observation pipelines
Phase 02

Subsystem demonstrations

Mature critical components such as timing transfer, receiver chains, inter-satellite links, onboard storage, and ground processing workflows.

  • Clock and metrology tests
  • Receiver and calibration prototypes
  • Data transport experiments
Phase 03

Pathfinder mission

Fly a focused demonstration to validate formation-flying operations, baseline reconstruction, signal handling, correlation, and imaging with real space data.

  • Two- or few-spacecraft architecture
  • End-to-end interferometric validation
  • Operational lessons for scaling
Phase 04

Observatory-scale array

Scale toward a multi-spacecraft facility capable of routine science operations, community access, calibrated data products, and long-term observatory support.

  • Expanded spacecraft network
  • Science operations model
  • Public data and imaging products
